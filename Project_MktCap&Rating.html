For feature selection and model comparison, I want to transfer the S&P Rating into numerical variable, after I get the excel file of S&P Rating, I develop a easy an reasonable methods to do this: we firstly count the number of occurrences of each type of Rating and convert it into percentage, then, from 0 to 1, we allocate the [0,1] space to every rating from low to high and the allocation size is depending on the percentage. For example, the percentage of lowest rating "CC" is 2%, then [0,2%] is for "CC", the next higher rating is "CCC-" with percentage 5%, then [2%,7%] is for "CCC-"... then we use the middle point of each space as the numerical value of the corresponding Rating.(I did these in the "Rating.elsx")

I generate a idea to fill in these missing point: generally speaking, among all financial variables, the ratio-like variables are mostly industry-relevant, meaning we can group the dataset by different industries and use the average value of each group to fill in the missing values in this group.And since some variables have few missing points, we can also use this method to fill in the missing point for this kind of variables.

group_df=df.groupby('industry')

    for FactorName in IndustryRelated:
        temp_df[FactorName].fillna(temp_df[FactorName].median(),inplace=True)
    df.update(temp_df)

Then we deal with the remaining missing points, the variable "foreign scr" miss many values, as a reasonable assumption, we think it is like a deposit or insurance money in the foreign countries, so the reason for the missing points is many companies do not have a multinational business. So in this column, we will in the blank with 0. In the ramaining variables like "cash", "debt" and "growth rate", besides the industry, they are also relevant to the company size, such as big company always has more money and lower growth rate. So according to the online reference, we divide the sample into "L","M","S" groups, and use two dimensions-industry and company size to group the sample and use the average value of each group to fill in the missing point.

Reference URL:https://financialengines.com/education-center/small-large-mid-caps-market-capitalization/

Analysis

The results are not bad, the average score for Elastic Net, Neutral Network, SVM and Random Forest are approximately 0.67, 0.67, 0.64 and 0.65 respectively. However, unlike a classification problem, it is hard to interpret these scores. What does score of 0.67 mean? Is it good or bad? Without the meaning of these scores, we hardly can draw out our conclusions.

Therefore, I generate a new method to evaluate these models. With the predicted data and real data, I create a index representing the prediction bias: abs(abs(prediction)-abs(real value))/abs(real value). For each model, we count the percentage of samples with prediction bias less than 10%, 20%, 30% and 40% respectively. You could say, the prediction with 40% bias is not a precise prediction. However, the prediction with 40% bias is still in the same order of magnitude with the real value, resulting in the comapny with predicted value probably can be divided into the same group of similar company size with the company with real value, which indicated 40% bias is still a meaningful threshold for evaluating these models.

Let's see the following result, the x label is the prediction bias threshold, y label is the percentage of the number of samples. So (10%,70%) means, on average, with this model, the samples with prediction bias less than 10% account for 90% of all samples.
